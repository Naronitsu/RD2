\begin{abstract}
Safety and cybersecurity assurance for cyber-physical and IoT-like automotive systems requires lightweight verification that fits resource-constrained ECUs. This research designs and analytically evaluates a minimal, resource-bounded runtime verification monitor for a safety-critical automotive ECU. The design draws on automotive cybersecurity V\&V practice, CPS/IoT runtime verification techniques, and hardware-anchored trust and attestation. We derive a method for selecting and formalising a small set of ECU-local properties as automata or algebraic specifications, propose selective observation of events and messages to minimise overhead, and outline how hardware trust anchors and remote/hybrid attestation can protect the monitor in constrained environments. The outcome is a focused design blueprint for minimal runtime monitors that can provide meaningful safety and cybersecurity coverage without violating real-time and resource constraints.
\end{abstract}
